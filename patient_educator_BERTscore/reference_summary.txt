The main types of cardiomyopathy are defined by their physical effects on the heart muscle:

Dilated Cardiomyopathy (DCM): The most common form. The muscle of the left ventricle becomes stretched, thin (dilated), and weak. This prevents the heart from pumping blood effectively.

Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM): The heart muscle becomes abnormally thick (hypertrophied). This thickening makes it harder for the heart to fill with blood and can sometimes obstruct blood flow out of the heart.

Restrictive Cardiomyopathy (RCM): A less common type where the heart muscle becomes stiff and rigid. While the heart's pumping ability may be normal, the stiffness prevents it from relaxing and filling with blood properly between beats.

Arrhythmogenic Cardiomyopathy (AC/ARVC): A rare type where the muscle tissue of the right ventricle is replaced by scar tissue and fat. This can disrupt the heart's electrical system and cause dangerous arrhythmias.

Unclassified Cardiomyopathy: Includes other types of cardiomyopathy that do not fit into the other categories, such as stress-induced cardiomyopathy (Takotsubo) or left ventricular non-compaction.
