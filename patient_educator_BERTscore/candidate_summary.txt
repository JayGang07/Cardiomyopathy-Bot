There are several main types of cardiomyopathy, each affecting the heart muscle in a distinct way:
Dilated Cardiomyopathy (DCM): This is the most common type. In DCM, the heart's main pumping chamber, the left ventricle, becomes enlarged (dilated) and weakened. This stretching and thinning of the muscle make it less able to contract forcefully, resulting in reduced pumping efficiency. Over time, all four chambers of the heart can become enlarged. It often leads to heart failure.
Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM): In HCM, the heart muscle, particularly the walls of the ventricles (often the left ventricle and the septum between the ventricles), becomes abnormally thick (hypertrophied). This thickening can make it difficult for the heart to pump blood effectively and can also impede the heart's ability to relax and fill with blood properly between beats. In some cases, the thickened muscle can block blood flow out of the heart.
Restrictive Cardiomyopathy (RCM): This is a less common type where the heart muscle becomes stiff and rigid, but the walls may not be significantly thickened or dilated. The stiffness prevents the ventricles from relaxing fully and filling with blood between heartbeats, even though the pumping function (contraction) might initially be normal. This leads to reduced blood flow to the body.
Arrhythmogenic Right Ventricular Cardiomyopathy (ARVC/ARVD): In ARVC, the muscle tissue in the right ventricle (and sometimes the left) is gradually replaced by fatty and fibrous tissue. This replacement weakens the heart muscle and disrupts the heart's electrical signals, leading to irregular heart rhythms (arrhythmias) that can be life-threatening.
Unclassified Cardiomyopathy: This category is used for cardiomyopathies that don't fit neatly into one of the main types, or when they present with features of more than one type, or when the specific characteristics are not yet fully understood or defined.
